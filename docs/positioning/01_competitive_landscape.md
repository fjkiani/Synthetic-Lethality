## The Three Tiers of Competition

### Tier 1 — "Build New Drugs" (Not Our Competition)
**Recursion/Exscientia, Evariste, Repare, CoSyne, Insilico, Rakovina**

These companies start from scratch. They discover novel targets, design new molecules, and run their own clinical programs. They are capital-intensive ($754M cash for Recursion/Exscientia as of Q4 2025, after merger and program cuts), slow (10–15 years to market), and have produced zero FDA-approved drugs as of 2026 despite $60B+ in sector investment. They are not competing for the same assets we are targeting. They cannot do what we do because they are not reading the graveyard — they are building new graves.

**The Repare cautionary tale:** Repare is the closest scientific cousin — genome-wide CRISPR SL screens, precision oncology, licensing to pharma. Roche paid $125M upfront for camonsertib in 2022. Roche terminated the deal in 2024. Repare cut 25% of its workforce and out-licensed its entire discovery platform for $4M in May 2025. The lesson: being a drug developer with SL science is not the same as being an IP licensor with SL science. We are not Repare.

### Tier 2 — "Rescue Failed Drugs" (Closest Business Model Analogs)
**Biossil, Ignota Labs**

These are the companies doing the most similar work. But the failure modes they address are different from ours, and their business models require capital we don't need.

**Biossil** ($70M raised April 2026, backed by OpenAI + Founders Fund): Buys or licenses failed drug candidates, uses AI to find new indications, then runs the trial themselves. They are a drug developer. After 3 years of stealth operation, they have 10 molecules and 2 in advanced clinical trials. Their model requires acquiring the molecule, running the trial, and waiting for approval. That is a 5–10 year, $100M+ path per asset.

**Ignota Labs** ($6.9M seed, Cambridge UK): Fixes drugs that failed because they were toxic. Their SAFEPATH platform identifies the root cause of safety problems and re-engineers the molecule. They acquired Kronos Bio's pipeline (CDK9 + SYK inhibitors) in October 2025. Completely different failure mode — they fix toxic drugs; we fix wrong-patient drugs.

**The key distinction:** Both Biossil and Ignota own the molecule and run the trial. We own the indication + patient selection logic and license it back. We are asset-light. We do not need to acquire the drug, run the trial, or wait for approval. We file the patent, create the urgency, and license the map.

### Tier 3 — "Sell Platform Access" (Not Our Model)
**BenevolentAI, Oncolix.ai**

Platform licensing to pharma. They sell access to their AI. We are not selling access — we are selling de-risked assets with IP attached. These are fundamentally different value propositions.

## Why Patient Selection Is the Biggest Failure Mode

Of all the reasons drugs fail in clinical trials:
- **~40% fail on efficacy** — the drug doesn't work in the enrolled population
- **~30% fail on safety** — the drug is toxic
- **~20% fail on patient selection** — the drug works, but not in the patients enrolled
- **~10% fail on delivery** — the drug can't reach the target

Ignota Labs addresses the 30% safety failure segment. Biossil addresses part of the 40% efficacy failure segment (wrong indication). **CrisPRO addresses the 20% patient selection failure segment — the segment where the drug mechanism is validated, the safety profile exists, and the only missing piece is the correct patient population.**

This is the highest-value segment because:
1. The drug already has Phase I/II safety data — the most expensive part of development is done
2. The mechanism is validated — the drug works, just not in the enrolled population
3. The fix is a biomarker + patient selection rule — not a new molecule, not a new trial from scratch
4. The pharma company has already spent $150M–$500M — they are highly motivated to recover that investment

## The Competitive Tension Play: Why Both Buyers Need Us

**The original owner** (Merck/Roche/BMS) has already spent the money. They have the safety data, the manufacturing process, the Phase I/II data. The only thing missing is the patient selection logic. Licensing our IP is 10x cheaper than running a new trial from scratch. Their incentive: recover the sunk cost.

**The competitor** (any company running a similar mechanism in CNS) is about to make the same mistake. They are running an αV integrin program, a checkpoint program, or an mTOR program in brain cancer without the patient selection logic. Our patent creates a choice: license our biomarker and run a trial that works, or run the trial without it and fail for the same reason the original trial failed. Their incentive: not repeat a $150M mistake.

**The tension:** We approach the original owner first with a 60-day right of first negotiation. If they don't engage, we approach the competitor. Both parties know this. The patent clock is running. The urgency is structural, not manufactured.
